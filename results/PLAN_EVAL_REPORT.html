Where the Risks Concentrate
Five requirements are completely unaddressed. These are not edge cases — they define how the AI experience behaves and whether it meets product trust standards.
PRD-073 critical missing
Spoiler safety & domain restriction
Risk: Ask and Scoop can drift into spoilers or non-entertainment chat without this policy, violating the product's core trust boundary with users.
PRD-026 critical partial
Data sync continuity across upgrades
Risk: Merge primitives exist but duplicate handling, settings continuity, and upgrade-safe carry-forward are underspecified — risking silent data loss at version boundaries.
PRD-057 important missing
Retry on parse failure before fallback
Risk: Without a retry path, any parse error degrades immediately to a raw fallback, degrading the Ask experience for edge-case inputs.
PRD-074 important missing
Specificity bar for AI reasoning
Risk: No guard against generic genre boilerplate means AI reasoning passes silently while giving users vague, unhelpful explanations for why a show was recommended.
PRD-078 important missing
Discovery quality scoring rubric
Risk: Without an explicit rubric, there is no threshold or golden-set validation strategy. AI discovery quality becomes purely subjective and impossible to regression-test.

Assessment Narrative

This is a structurally sound implementation plan with meaningful holes — not a fundamentally broken one. The score is held back less by missing pages or CRUD flows than by under-specified behavioral contracts, especially where the PRD expects the AI experience to feel intentional rather than merely functional.

The plan is strongest in benchmark isolation, persistence scaffolding, and the main product topology. Infrastructure sections cover namespaces, user partitioning, row-level isolation, scripts, and migrations with concrete implementation detail. The Collection Home, Search, Show Detail, Person Detail, and Alchemy flows are all mapped into specific sections, endpoints, and verification steps — the plan is particularly strong on route-level coverage and data-backed user flows.

The gaps cluster around AI behavior and quality, plus a smaller set of durability semantics. On the AI side, the plan specifies request/response shapes and major flows, but largely treats voice, guardrails, and quality bars as implementation details. That shows up in missing or partial coverage for spoiler safety, domain restriction, direct-answer formatting, parse-retry fallback, surprise-without-betrayal, and the explicit discovery scoring rubric.

On the data side, the plan has merge logic and timestamps, but is thinner on client-cache disposability, upgrade continuity, duplicate merging, and local/UI persistence details. These are correctness risks, not missing features — the code paths exist, the behavioral contracts defining when and how they trigger are underspecified.

If this plan were executed as-is, the most likely failure mode is an app that looks complete on the surface but feels off-brand and brittle in discovery-heavy scenarios. A QA reviewer or stakeholder would probably notice Ask and Scoop first: responses could be technically wired up yet still sound generic, mishandle edge cases, skip required fallbacks, or violate spoiler/domain expectations.

The remaining planning work is mostly about specification depth, not whole new feature areas. The plan needs a dedicated AI behavior section that states persona, spoiler/domain guardrails, response-shape fallback behavior, and the quality rubric in first-class terms. It also needs a tighter data-durability section covering client-cache assumptions, duplicate merge behavior, upgrade continuity, and the exact local/UI persistence expectations that the technical reference calls out.
